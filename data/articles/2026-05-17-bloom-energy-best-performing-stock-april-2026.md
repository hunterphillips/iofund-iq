## Thesis
Bloom Energy's behind-the-meter solid oxide fuel cells uniquely solve AI's most critical constraint—time-to-power—rather than compute availability or software capability. The Oracle Project Jupiter deal (2.45 GW, 100% Bloom-powered campus) validates product-market fit, and the coming inference buildout in dense metros structurally favors Bloom's low-emission, modular, grid-independent profile.

## Key numbers
- BE April 2026 return: ~109% (best-performing large-cap during S&P's best month in 6 years)
- I/O Fund initial entries: $16.64–$17.04 (April 2025); ~1,300% return from those levels
- Q1 2026 revenue: $751M, +130% YoY; beat estimates by 39%
- Product revenue: $653M, +208% YoY (~87% of total)
- FY2026 guidance raised to $3.4B–$3.8B midpoint (~78% YoY growth)
- Adjusted gross margin: 31.5% (up from 28.7%); FY guidance raised to 34%
- Adjusted EPS: $0.44 vs. $0.13 estimate (+242% beat); FY guidance ~$2.05 (+170% YoY)
- First-ever positive Q1 operating cash flow: $73.6M vs. -$110.8M prior year
- Oracle deal: 2.8 GW total capacity, 1.2 GW shipping now; NOx emissions cut 92% vs. gas turbines
- Inference power demand CAGR: 122% through 2028 (BCG); McKinsey projects ~91 GW by 2030

## Decision-relevant takeaways
- Oracle's all-Bloom campus is a proof-of-concept that could catalyze similar wins; no other solution matched speed, emissions, or water efficiency.
- Inference deployments favor dense metros where grid queues are longest—Bloom's natural gas pipeline compatibility and small footprint are structural advantages competitors lack.
- New CFO Simon Edwards (ex-Groq CEO, acquired by Nvidia) signals deliberate positioning for the inference wave.
- PJM 6.6 GW capacity shortfall and ERCOT's 226 GW interconnection backlog reinforce urgency of behind-the-meter solutions.

## Risks / watch-fors
- Stock already up ~1,300% from initial entry; repeat outperformance requires continued large contract wins, not just favorable macro.
- Debt ($2.60B) slightly exceeds cash ($2.49B); cash flow inflection is new and unproven across multiple quarters.
- Analyst notes the next alpha opportunity lies in identifying the *next* bottleneck—implying Bloom's easy-money phase may be maturing.
- Fuel cell cost competitiveness depends on sustained double-digit annual cost reductions continuing as scale increases.
