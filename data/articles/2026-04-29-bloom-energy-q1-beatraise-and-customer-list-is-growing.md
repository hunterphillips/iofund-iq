## Thesis
Bloom Energy posted its strongest quarterly growth since going public, with revenue and EPS crushing estimates by wide margins, while simultaneously expanding margins and raising full-year guidance. The Oracle Project Jupiter deal—the first AI data center to run entirely on Bloom's fuel cells—validates the company's product-market fit, and management confirmed that hyperscalers and neoclouds beyond Oracle now represent more than half of the data center backlog.

## Key numbers
- Q1 revenue: $751M vs. $540M expected (+39% beat); +130% YoY
- Full-year 2026 revenue guide: $3.6B midpoint (raised from $3.2B); ~78% YoY growth
- Q1 adjusted EPS: $0.44 vs. $0.13 expected (+242% beat)
- Full-year adjusted EPS guide: $2.05 midpoint (+170% YoY, raised from +85%)
- Adjusted gross margin: 31.5% (full-year target raised to 34% from 32%)
- Adjusted EBITDA: $143M (19% margin vs. 7.7% a year ago)
- Operating margin: +15.4 points YoY to 9.6% GAAP
- Q1 operating cash flow: +$73.6M (first positive Q1 in company history)
- Oracle deal: 2.8GW total, 2.45GW for Project Jupiter in New Mexico
- Product revenue: $653M, +208% YoY (~87% of total revenue)

## Decision-relevant takeaways
- Project Jupiter is the first AI data center fully powered by Bloom; eliminates gas turbines and cuts NOx emissions 92% vs. prior plan.
- Hyperscalers and neoclouds beyond Oracle now account for >50% of data center backlog—customer concentration risk is declining.
- Service contracts carry a 100% attach rate and 10–15 year durations, creating a growing annuity revenue stream with improving margins.
- Inference workloads favor Bloom's small footprint, low emissions, and minimal water use—positioning the company well for dense metro deployments.
- Native DC output (800V architecture) is a structural catalyst expected to matter more in 2027–2028.

## Risks / watch-fors
- Construction pace at customer data centers could become a bottleneck even if Bloom itself is not capacity-constrained.
- Scaling to multi-GW annual deployments requires substantial working capital well ahead of revenue recognition; capital raises may be needed, though customer prepayments are partially offsetting this.
